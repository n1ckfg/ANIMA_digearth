A feature film essay on Aldous Huxley's cultural criticism and social prophecy in light of the approaching millennium.

An essential film regarding the history of technology and its social effects.

A contemporary reading of Huxley's oeuvre, a rendition and interpretation, inspired by an immersion into his life and thought...complex,iconoclastic, psychedelic, and historical.

Aldous Huxley: The Gravity Of Light incorporates archival footage, computer rendered 3D animation, speculative fictions, and selections from his essays.

The film begins by reflecting upon that crucial and prophetic work "Brave New World" (1932, Aldous Huxley) and then moves to a further inquiry into the human ramifications of current technological change. The concentration here is on the questions posed by the binary-code revolution. The film acknowledges, critically examines and attempts to, in poetic, visionary, and sometimes humorous language to explicate the exceptional potentials of the New Media Order.

David Odhiambo, a black actor, acts as a surrogate voice, an 'other' inflection that brings to the fore the difference between human reality and the cultural erasures inherent in cyberspace.


Personal in tone, the film also recalls the impact of Huxley's LSD-25 and mescaline experimentations and writings for a whole generation of youth and examines the utopianistic impulses associated with the recent Rave scene. The work reflects the aesthetics and poesis of the psychedelic state without collapsing into the tie-dye cliches that have trivialized the '60's era.

The film weaves together this material with sections from a 1957 CBC interview with Aldous Huxley, and footage shot in Los Angeles on May 1, 1994 at the Huxley Symposium. Doctor Jean Houston, a senior advisor to the United Nations on matters of Human Development, eloquently speaks on the immense contribution Huxley made concerning the possible human.
